# Public Archive Reading Bible

**System:** finite, beat-bound public records

**Scope:** 60 records, one opening after each canonical campaign beat

**Completion policy:** acknowledge every paragraph once per save; no reward, score, ownership claim, or replay loop

**Shipped volume:** 60 records, 498 substantial paragraphs, and 31,163 counted visible words

## Narrative purpose

The campaign ends by opening an archive rather than installing a single victorious account. The reading table makes that idea playable throughout the journey. Each new story beat can open one fictional record that preserves an affected community's method, correction, disagreement, custody rule, or practical consequence.

These records are not hidden collectibles. A player does not remove an object from a persecuted household, convert testimony into currency, or receive completion power. The interface presents public copies under their stated access conditions. Private details can remain restricted inside the fiction, and every record names an accountable custodian.

## Canonical plan

The plan follows all 60 campaign beats in exact order and divides production into three contiguous groups of 20:

| Pack | Canonical slots | Role |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Early | 1–20 | altered orders, local shelter methods, ferry evidence, and the Takamine break |
| Middle | 21–40 | port testimony, forge chains, aqueduct routes, and resistance to court categories |
| Late | 41–60 | observatory evidence, public reconstruction, contested memory, and archive governance |

Each record binds an ID, global sequence, chapter, and exact unlock beat. Content packs may author prose but cannot change those identities or introduce another pacing claim.

## Record contract

Every record contains exactly:

- canonical ID, sequence, chapter, and unlock beat;
- a concrete multi-word title;
- a descriptive record type;
- a specific accountable custodian;
- a meaningful public-access, restriction, review, or correction note;
- 8–12 substantial paragraphs;
- at least 500 counted words across the complete visible record.

The validator rejects wrong order, unknown beats, extra reward or minute fields, duplicate IDs, exact repeated text, thin paragraphs, placeholder language, borrowed franchise references, real historical officials or performer likenesses, collectible suffering, and sacred objects framed as loot.

## Player flow

The Camp reading table lists records whose exact story beat is complete, plus the currently active reading. Beginning a record creates one finite progress entry. Each input acknowledges exactly one visible paragraph. The access note and custodian remain visible throughout. The final paragraph marks the reading complete and refuses replay mutation.

Only one record may be active at a time. This keeps save frontiers simple and ensures a browser page change cannot create two partial records. Completed records remain visible for review, but review must not increment completion or duration state.

## Duration boundary

The quantity model counts the exact visible title, record type, custodian, access note, and paragraphs. It adds one explicit start interaction and one line-advance input per paragraph. It does not use a declared reading duration, unseen prose, rewards, idle time, or repeat loops.

This arithmetic remains a model. A public record may be skimmed, reread, discussed, or ignored. All 60 records are part of the 215-activity intended route, including the record unlocked by the final beat. The receipt therefore remains active for that post-story, pre-credits reading and the credits gate refuses to seal until it and the other 214 activities are complete. Only a clean human run that explicitly completes credits and records at least 1,200 active minutes on that same receipt can prove the 20-hour target.

## Editorial review checklist

- Does the record preserve a distinct practical fact or disagreement rather than paraphrase the scene that unlocked it?
- Is the custodian accountable, and can affected people correct or restrict the copy?
- Is the prose form appropriate—ledger annotation, route report, council minute, letter, transcript, repair map, or another specific public form?
- Does the record avoid making one protagonist the owner of community memory?
- Are uncertainty, method, and withheld details visible where relevant?
- Does sentence structure and rhetorical cadence differ from neighboring records?
- Are real victims, officials, performers, and borrowed characters absent?
- Are faith, persecution, illness, death, and survival treated as lived conditions rather than atmosphere or collectible content?

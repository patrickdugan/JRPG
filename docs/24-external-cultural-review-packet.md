# External Historical and Cultural Review Packet

Status: **ready for independent review; no external approval recorded.**

This packet is the bounded handoff for a Japanese cultural historian, a scholar of early-modern Japanese Christianity, and Japanese sensitivity readers. It does not ask a reviewer to approve combat balance, software quality, or elapsed playtime.

## Review build

- Git commit: record the reviewed commit at handoff; never reuse findings after relevant script or art changes without a delta review.
- Historical frame: alternate Genna 8 / 1622 after the 1614 ban.
- Narrative volume: 60 canonical scenes plus ten Storyworld decisions and ten played consequences per route.
- Visual scope: 20 scene panoramas, 16-role/32-frame NPC field suite, party/enemy/boss atlases, field overlays, battle boards, VFX, and generated production-reference sheets.
- Required play surfaces: Campaign, Camp conversations/councils/archive, Battle context cards, and Credits.

## Independent questions

Each reviewer should mark every question `accept`, `revise`, `outside scope`, or `not reviewed`, then cite the exact scene, line, frame, or asset.

1. Do Sayo and other Japanese organizers retain authority to set routes, consent rules, testimony handling, risk limits, and refusal without needing European validation?
2. Does the script distinguish clandestine clergy from Japanese lay communities rather than presenting an open post-1614 foreign mission?
3. Does Mateus's accountability arc avoid absolution by romance, suffering, foreignness, or a single heroic act?
4. Are shrine, temple, and Christian practices represented without imposing a modern exclusive religious binary on early-modern communities?
5. Does any prop, item, puzzle, VFX, environment, or enemy design turn a sacred or devotional object into loot, neutral gothic decoration, or spectacle?
6. Does the contested `Ashen Oni` label remain visibly fictional and avoid copying a sacred figure, ceremonial mask, identifiable local tradition, or ethnicity-coded monster class?
7. Are titles, personal names, place-name sounds, clothing layers, work tools, domestic spaces, port activity, and travel routes plausible enough for the stated alternate setting?
8. Does Kurohana’s explicitly fictional mass processional of crucified and impaled Kirishitan victims remain attached to Kurozane’s state violence, two living rescues, chosen names, and evidence custody without recreating identifiable historical martyrs or presenting Japanese Christianity itself as macabre? Are the content warning and planned reduced-horror empty-structure variants sufficient?
9. Is the Black Chrysanthemum clearly Kurozane's theft and defacement rather than an authentic shogunal or imperial emblem?
10. Do visual silhouettes for the twelve community roles read as distinct people with practical local authority rather than interchangeable exotic background figures?
